Methodology

How this works

1. Per-marker GMM 2. Population clustering 3. Outlier flagging 4. Correlations

Per-marker Gaussian mixture model

What we fit. A Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) is fit to each marker independently. The model assumes the observed values come from a mixture of K bell-shaped (Gaussian) components, each with its own mean µ, standard deviation σ, and mixing weight w (the weights sum to 1).

How we choose K. For each marker we fit K = 1, 2, … up to a cap of min(4, max(2, n // 5)) where n is the number of tests for that marker. We pick the K with the lowest Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC): BIC = −2·log L + k·log n, where L is the maximum likelihood and k the number of free parameters. Lower BIC = better fit, with a penalty for complexity. We only adopt a K > 1 solution when its BIC beats K=1 by at least 6 BIC units — the conventional "strong evidence" threshold — otherwise we report a single Gaussian. This stops the view from inventing groups in genuinely uniform markers.

Library and parameters. sklearn.mixture.GaussianMixture(n_components=K, n_init=5, random_state=42). Five random restarts mitigate local optima; the random seed makes results reproducible. The marker-by-marker BIC scores for the currently shown marker are listed in the per-marker Technical details below the chart.

Group boundaries and labels. Hard labels for each test come from the GMM's posterior assignments (gmm.predict), then remapped into mean-sorted order so "Group 1" is always the lowest-mean cluster. The boundary lines drawn on the chart are the x-values where adjacent components' weighted PDFs cross, found with scipy.optimize.brentq (midpoint fallback if no crossing exists). They approximate the posterior decision rule visually but do not drive the labels themselves.

Worked example

The data. Eight fictional fasting-glucose readings (mmol/L): 4.8, 5.1, 5.2, 5.4, 7.9, 8.1, 8.3, 8.6. n = 8.

1. Fit K = 1. One Gaussian over all eight points: µ = 6.675, σ = 1.568. Free parameters k = 2 (µ, σ²). log L = −14.95. BIC1 = −2·(−14.95) + 2·log(8) = 29.90 + 4.16 = 34.06.

2. Fit K = 2. With well-separated points, EM converges to:

  • Group 1: µ1 = 5.125, σ1 = 0.217, w1 = 0.500
  • Group 2: µ2 = 8.225, σ2 = 0.259, w2 = 0.500

Free parameters k = 5 (two means, two variances, one mixing weight; the second weight is fixed by 1 − w1). log L = −5.37. BIC2 = −2·(−5.37) + 5·log(8) = 10.73 + 10.40 = 21.13.

3. Apply the rule. ΔBIC = 34.06 − 21.13 = 12.93. 12.93 ≥ 6, so we adopt K = 2.

4. Label a new test. For a hypothetical reading at x = 6.5 mmol/L the weighted component densities are:

  • w1·N(6.5 | 5.125, 0.217) ≈ 1.6 × 10−9
  • w2·N(6.5 | 8.225, 0.259) ≈ 1.7 × 10−10

The posterior is the ratio: P(Group 1 | x = 6.5) ≈ 0.91, P(Group 2 | x = 6.5) ≈ 0.09. The new test is labelled Group 1.

For a dataset point well inside its cluster (e.g. x = 5.4), the posterior is decisive: P(Group 1 | x = 5.4) ≈ 1.0 to six decimal places. Tight, separated clusters give confident assignments; values near the cross-over between components produce intermediate posteriors and flag boundary cases on the Outliers tab.

Numbers above match sklearn.mixture.GaussianMixture(n_components=K, n_init=5, random_state=42) fit to the eight values.

Assumptions and limitations.

  • Each marker is treated as 1-dimensional and independent of the others (the Clusters tab handles the multivariate case).
  • A mixture of Gaussians is a good fit for many clinical markers, but heavily skewed or bounded distributions can be over-fit to multiple components.
  • With small samples (< 30 tests) the choice between 2 and 3 components can be unstable. Small-sample warnings are surfaced explicitly in the headline.
  • The model uses no clinical reference range as input. Reference lines on the chart are added afterwards purely as context.

Population clustering (PCA → GMM)

Pipeline.

  1. Pivot long-format data to wide (rows = blood tests, columns = markers).
  2. Drop markers where > 50% of tests have a missing value.
  3. Median-impute the remaining missing values.
  4. Standardise (zero mean, unit variance per marker) with StandardScaler.
  5. Reduce dimensionality with Principal Component Analysis (PCA). We keep the smallest number of principal components whose cumulative explained variance ≥ 80% (minimum 2).
  6. Fit a GMM in that reduced space with K = 1, 2, …, up to a sample-size cap of max(2, min(5, n // 25)). Pick the K with the lowest BIC; only adopt K > 1 if its BIC beats K=1 by at least 6 units.
  7. For each test, store the hard cluster label, the full posterior probability vector, the log-likelihood, and the squared Mahalanobis distance to the assigned cluster's mean — the last two feed the Outliers tab.

Library and parameters. sklearn.decomposition.PCA(random_state=42) for dimensionality reduction; sklearn.mixture.GaussianMixture(n_components=K, covariance_type='diag', n_init=5, random_state=42) for the cluster fit. Diagonal covariance is appropriate because PCA already decorrelates the global data; full covariance over- penalises K > 1 in small cohorts (~5× more parameters per cluster). The K cap and ΔBIC=6 floor together prevent over-clustering on cohorts of fewer than ~125 patients.

The 2-D scatter. The chart shows the first two principal components. Clustering happens in the higher-dimensional reduced space, not on the 2-D view — so two tests can sit close together on the chart but belong to different clusters if they differ on axes that aren't shown.

  • Group 1: µ = (−1.776, +0.141), σ² = (0.015, 0.002), w = 0.500 — tests T1, T2, T3

Mahalanobis² to Group 2's mean (with diagonal covariance, this is just Σ (xᵢ − µᵢ)² / σᵢ²):

  • PC1 term: (2.386 − 1.776)² / 0.208 = 0.372 / 0.208 ≈ 1.79
  • PC2 term: (1.542 − (−0.141))² / 1.418 = 2.832 / 1.418 ≈ 2.00
  • Sum ≈ 3.78

The χ² critical value at p = 0.01 with df = 2 is 9.21, so T5 is not flagged as a multivariate outlier — Group 2's elevated PC2 variance (1.418) absorbs the anomaly precisely because the cluster is doing the right thing: modelling the within-group spread. This is why the Outliers tab uses the fitted model rather than a fixed distance threshold.

Why diagonal covariance? For K = 2, free-parameter counts grow steeply with full covariance:

DimensionsDiagonalFullΔ
2 (this toy fit)911+2
52141+20
10 (~ demo cohort scale)41131+90

Counts come from K·D means + K·D variances (diagonal) or K·D(D+1)/2 covariance entries (full), plus K−1 mixing weights. Each extra parameter is penalised by k · log(n) in BIC. In 10-D space full covariance adds ~90 free parameters per K = 2 model; on a cohort of n = 80 that's an extra penalty of 90 · log(80) ≈ 394 BIC units. Real two-cluster structure can't beat that penalty on small cohorts, so full-covariance K = 2 systematically loses to K = 1. Diagonal covariance restores BIC's ability to find genuine structure when n < ~125.

All numbers above are the exact output of analyse_population() on this toy dataset: StandardScaler → PCA(random_state=42) → GaussianMixture(covariance_type='diag', n_init=5, random_state=42).

Assumptions and limitations.

  • PCA assumes the structure in the data is approximately linear.
  • GMM assumes Gaussian-shaped clusters in the reduced space; very non-Gaussian shapes (e.g. concentric rings, manifolds) will be misfit.
  • The 80% variance threshold is conventional but arbitrary; raising it would use more dimensions and potentially split into more clusters.
  • With K up to 5 and small samples (< 200 tests) the model has plenty of freedom and may over-cluster. The headline carries a small-sample warning when applicable.

Outlier flagging

Two flagging rules. Both rules use outputs of the cluster model on the Clusters tab — nothing is re-fit here.

1. Boundary cases. The cluster model assigns each test a posterior probability per cluster (the probabilities sum to 1). We flag tests whose maximum posterior is below 0.7, meaning no single cluster claims more than 70% of the test's profile — the remainder is split between two or more clusters. The "Why investigate" line names the top two competing clusters and their probabilities.

2. Multivariate outliers. For each test we compute the squared Mahalanobis distance to its assigned cluster's mean in the PCA-reduced space: d² = Σ (xᵢ−μᵢ)² / σᵢ². Under the model this follows a χ² distribution with df = n_cluster_dims degrees of freedom, so we flag tests whose d² exceeds the χ² critical value at p = 0.01 (scipy.stats.chi2.ppf(0.99, df)). This is an absolute threshold — if no test is genuinely unusual, none are flagged. The previous quantile-based rule (bottom 5% of log-likelihood) always flagged ~5% of tests by construction and has been retired.

Confidence and sorting. The Confidence column is the maximum posterior probability, expressed as a percentage. Rows are sorted ascending by Confidence so the tests the model is least sure about appear first.

Worked example — boundary case

Suppose a K = 3 cluster model assigned a test Tb the following posterior probability vector:

ClusterPosterior
Cluster 10.55
Cluster 20.40
Cluster 30.05

The probabilities sum to 1.00. The hard label is argmax → Cluster 1. The maximum posterior is 0.55, which is below the 0.7 threshold, so Tb is flagged as a boundary case.

The "Why investigate" line is built from the top two posteriors:

  • 55% Cluster 1 · 40% Cluster 2

The third cluster is omitted (only the two competing components matter for the investigation prompt). Confidence in the table is round(0.55 · 100) = 55%.

Worked example — multivariate outlier

Now consider a different test To that does land confidently in a cluster (max posterior > 0.7, so the boundary rule does not flag it). Suppose the cluster model kept three principal components and assigned To to Cluster 1, with:

  • To in PCA-reduced space: x = (4.5, −2.0, 1.5)
  • Cluster 1 mean: µ = (1.5, −0.5, 0.5)
  • Cluster 1 diagonal covariance: σ² = (0.6, 0.4, 0.5)

With diagonal covariance, the squared Mahalanobis distance reduces to a sum of squared z-scores:

Axis(xᵢ − µᵢ)²÷ σᵢ²Contribution
PC1(3.0)² = 9.00÷ 0.615.00
PC2(−1.5)² = 2.25÷ 0.45.625
PC3(1.0)² = 1.00÷ 0.52.00
Sum22.625

The χ² critical value at p = 0.01 with df = 3 is scipy.stats.chi2.ppf(0.99, df=3) ≈ 11.345. Since 22.625 > 11.345, To is flagged as a multivariate outlier: under the assumed Gaussian-mixture model, only ~1% of tests should sit this far from their assigned cluster's mean by chance.

PC1 dominates (15.00 of the 22.625), so To's anomaly is concentrated on the most explanatory axis — if PC1 captured, say, an overall metabolic-load score, this test is unusually far from its cluster on that axis.

Why the old 5% rule was retired

Previously the Outliers tab flagged the tests in the bottom 5% of log-likelihood within each cluster. That rule always flags ~5% of the cohort by construction: on a uniform cohort with no genuine outliers it still surfaces five of every hundred tests, which is misleading. The χ² rule above is an absolute threshold — if no test is genuinely unusual, none are flagged. On a cohort with many genuine outliers (e.g. heavy-tailed marker distributions), it flags more than 1%; on a clean Gaussian cohort, it flags close to 1%; on a uniform cohort with no outliers, it flags zero.

Assumptions and limitations.

  • The χ² threshold is calibrated under the Gaussian-mixture assumption. If a cluster's true within-cluster distribution is non-Gaussian (heavy-tailed, skewed), expected outlier rates may differ from the nominal 1%.
  • The 0.7 boundary threshold is a heuristic. Tighter (0.6) catches more boundary cases; looser (0.8) fewer.
  • Both rules inherit the assumptions of the cluster model itself: approximately linear structure (PCA), diagonal within-cluster covariance, and Gaussian-shaped clusters (GMM).
  • Diagonal covariance ignores within-cluster correlation between PCs; if real clusters are elongated along non-axis directions, the Mahalanobis distance under-states actual distance and outliers may be missed.

Correlations

Metric. For the selected pair (X, Y) we compute the Pearson correlation coefficient r across all blood tests with non-missing values for both markers. r ranges from −1 (perfect inverse linear relationship) through 0 (no linear relationship) to +1 (perfect direct linear relationship).

Library and parameters. pandas.DataFrame.corr(min_periods=10) — we require at least 10 overlapping observations for r to be reported. The starting pair is the one with the largest |r| in the data.

Reference range overlay. The dotted green lines on the chart show the upper bound of the canonical normal range for each marker (from the project's reference table; currently male ranges only). They are context, not input — they are not used in the correlation calculation.

Counter-example — what r misses

Consider ten paired readings with a clear non-linear relationship: y = x² (a perfect parabola, skipping x = 0 to keep ten points):

x−5−4−3−2−1+1+2+3+4+5
y25169411491625

Pearson r on this pair is exactly 0.000: every positive deviation in x is matched by an equal-and-opposite partner, so all the products cancel. The metric reads "no relationship" even though x and y are deterministically tied (knowing x gives you y exactly).

This is the headline caveat of Pearson r: it captures linear association only. The pair could be a U-shape, a sine wave, or any non-monotonic curve and r would still register near zero.

Spearman's rank correlation would catch monotonic non-linear relationships (e.g. y = ex, where y rises with x at an increasing rate — not a straight line, but order-preserving). Spearman computes Pearson on the ranks rather than the raw values, so any monotonic transform scores r = 1. It does not rescue the U-shape above — that's non-monotonic, and Spearman returns 0 too. For non-monotonic dependence, neither metric is the right tool; visual inspection or distance correlation is.

Assumptions and limitations.

  • Pearson r captures linear association only. Non-linear coupling (e.g. U-shaped) will read as weak even when a real relationship exists. Spearman's rank correlation would be more appropriate for monotonic non-linear cases.
  • Outliers can dominate r in small cohorts; the metric is not robust.
  • Correlation does not imply causation. Two markers can correlate because they're both driven by a third underlying factor (e.g. liver function affecting several enzymes).
  • The "strongest pair" auto-pick is based on |r| alone, not on biological plausibility or statistical significance.
